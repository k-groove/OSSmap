RETHINK DISCIPLINE

Who gets suspended? Where are there disparities? What is happening to our students?

  • — U.S. Department of Education

Powered by InformED and the 2013-14 Civil Rights Data Collection.

Suspensions shut out students

The practice of disciplining students by suspending them from school begins in preschool and continues through high school. School discipline is a complex and difficult issue. Schools and districts have an obligation to keep campuses safe. But research has found that very few suspensions are for acts of violence or violations of statutes. Schools also have an obligation to create environments that promote learning for all students. Excluding students from school and denying them the opportunity to learn can have devastating, long-term effects.

We know that students who are suspended are more likely to be retained in grade or to drop out, and we know exclusionary discipline feeds the school-to-prison pipeline.

The Department of Education recently released new data from the 2013–14 Civil Rights Data Collection (CRDC) survey that help shed light on who is suspended and where and how often suspensions are imposed. The survey data also show that students of color were disproportionately affected by expulsions and referrals to law enforcement. View the full CRDC dataset. This analysis examines only out-of-school suspensions.

Despite progress, suspensions are still widespread

We are seeing progress as more and more schools find alternative ways of addressing non-violent discipline issues. The total number of students suspended has fallen by about 20 percent since the CRDC data collection in 2011–12. However this progress is not occurring in all areas, or for all groups of students. Who is suspended?

This tool illuminates the scope of the problem as well as which students are most affected. School communities can use it as a resource as they think through the educational impact of suspensions and expulsions and whether systems of support can be set up for students and educators that will reduce the need for such measures.

Overall, about 5 percent of students received at least one out-of-school suspension in 2013–14. Thirteen percent of black students were suspended, the highest rate of any group of students and more than twice the national average. American Indian students were the next mostly likely to be suspended, at 8 percent. The least likely to be suspended were Asian students, at less than 2 percent.

See suspension rates for other student groups in the next chart.

View Chart By:

Though males are overall more likely to be suspended than females, how much more varies by race and ethnicity. The purple dot shows the percentage of females who were suspended, the fuschia dot the percentage of males suspended, while the line between shows the difference.

Seventeen percent of black males were suspended out of school at least once. The rate for black females was 9 percent, showing that black males were almost 8 percentage points more likely to receive suspensions than black females. Hispanic males were nearly 4 percentage points more likely than Hispanic females to be suspended, and the suspension rate for white males was 3 percentage points higher than for white females.

When broken out further, by disability status, the data diverge even more. Though black males make up only 12 percent of those identified with disabilities, they make up 26 percent of students with disabilities who were suspended.

Suspending over 6,000 of our youngest students

Preschool is a critical and important time and sets the foundation for our life-long learning. Shutting out and suspending 3-, 4-, and 5-year-olds from preschool disrupts their time for learning and development. This is why the U.S. Department of Education, in conjunction with the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, has called on schools and programs to do all they can to prevent or severely limit out-of-school suspensions for our youngest learners. There is urgency to this work; school district preschool programs suspended over 6,000 preschool students in 2013–14. Even at this early age, black students are disproportionately affected by out-of-school suspension.

View Chart By:

Nearly half of all suspended preschoolers were black and 1 in 5 was Hispanic. Additionally, though white students make up 42 percent of the preschool population, they were 28 percent of those suspended. The data show that suspended preschoolers were overwhelmingly male. The data also show that children with disabilities and English learners were less likely to be suspended as it relates to their enrollment.

Digging deeper into these data shows that there are differences by gender within each race and ethnicity as well. The graph below shows whether each group is overrepresented or underrepresented when suspensions are compared to enrollments.

Black males had the highest rate of overrepresentation. Black male preschoolers make up less than 10 percent of the population but more than 34 percent of suspended preschoolers, indicating that they are overrepresented in suspensions by nearly 25 percentage points. Black females make up 9 percent of the preschool population but 12 percent of suspended students, indicating that they are overrepresented in the suspension population by 3 percentage points.

This disproportionate impact is one of the many reasons this Administration—through the President’s My Brother’s Keeper initiative as well as the Department’s #RethinkDiscipline campaign and the Department’s partnership with the White House Council on Women and Girls—has made reforming school discipline one of its top priorities. For example, the Department of Education is working with the Georgetown Center on Law and Poverty and the White House Council on Women and Girls to host a conference in fall 2016 on improving school systems’ responses to trauma in girls of color.

Demanding Progress

Educators will need to ask themselves difficult questions, such as: Could socioemotional supports like counseling or mentoring relationships make a difference? Could we strengthen instruction to achieve better engagement or more appropriate differentiation? Could training on issues of implicit bias or culturally relevant pedagogy change the outcome of a particular interaction? Could we engage parents or family members more effectively as partners? Could more learning time yield a better outcome than exclusion from the learning environment?

Schools should motivate students to want to do their best, to support their classmates, to give back to their community, and to communicate in ways big and small to our students and educators that their potential is unlimited. In general, punitive approaches not only do not control behavior, they do not lead to better learning. Additionally, out-of-school suspensions can also contribute to chronic absenteeism. View more data from the CRDC on who is mostly likely to be chronically absent. We all have a role to play as we #rethinkdiscipline.

ENDNOTES

Data Exclusion Rules

Data included in this story may differ slightly from other published reports due to certain data decisions. For the purposes of these analyses, schools with incomplete or inconsistent data values were excluded. Less than 5 percent of schools were excluded for the following reasons:

  1. The school reported incomplete data for enrollment and/or the count of students suspended for at least one race/ethnicity subgroup.
  2. The count of students suspended was larger than the school enrollment for at least one race/ethnicity subgroup. Schools with partial or missing data for English learners were included in all analyses except those that focused on these subgroups.

Preschool Definition

The definition of preschool used in the Civil Rights Data Collection (CRDC) includes preschool programs and services for children ages 3 through 5.

Map Notes

A district might have missing data on the map if:

  1. It did not report suspension data or the data were inconsistent.
  2. It did not match with the NCES district boundary file.
  3. Twenty-five percent of its enrollment was in schools with incomplete or inconsistent data (see Data Exclusion Rules).

District data displayed on the map may not be based on all schools in the district. All districts with data have complete and consistent data for at least 75 percent of their enrollment.

On this map, the label Elementary includes all school districts that are financially responsible for providing education only to children in the elementary grades. Some elementary districts share territory with one or more Secondary districts. The label Independent Elementary refers to those Elementary school districts that are geographically independent of other school districts.

The label Secondary includes all school districts that are financially responsible for providing education only to children in the upper grades. This includes pseudo-secondary school districts created by the Census Bureau.

The label Unified refers to districts that provide education to children of all school ages. In general, where there is a unified school district, no elementary or secondary school district exists.

Other Technical Notes

This report uses three different types of percentages: a suspension rate, an enrollment composition percent, and a suspension composition percent.

  • The suspension rate is the number of students suspended divided by the number of students enrolled. A suspension rate is calculated overall and for each subgroup.
  • The enrollment composition percent is the number of subgroup students enrolled divided by the total number of students enrolled.
  • The suspension composition percent is the number of subgroup students suspended divided by the total number of students suspended.

When calculating the suspension rate for students with disabilities in each racial/ethnic group, suppressed enrollment counts were imputed to zero. Enrollment by race/ethnicity for students with disabilities was suppressed for privacy protection when the count was fewer than three (including zero).
